# Third Place
### Your personal coffee shop operating system

*One-pager — April 14, 2026*

---

## The Problem

3 million Americans treat coffee shops as their office — spending $2,000+ a year doing it. But they manage it with Google Maps and mental notes. Foursquare, the only app that gave people a personal relationship with their third places, shut down in December 2024. Discovery tools like Yelp and Workfrom tell you where to go — but they have no memory, no taste profile, and no habit loop. Every morning is the same low-grade friction: where should I work from today?

---

## The Solution

Third Place is a personal coffee shop operating system for people who work from cafés intentionally. It knows your taste, remembers everywhere you've been, spins the wheel when you can't decide, and keeps finding new spots that match your preferences — so you can focus on the work, not the logistics of getting there.

---

## Target Customer

**The Intentional Remote** — age 28–45, freelancer, consultant, or remote employee at a tech-adjacent company. Lives in a major metro. Genuine schedule flexibility 3+ days/week. Makes $80K–$180K. Thinks of themselves as someone with taste — follows coffee roasters on Instagram, knows the difference between third wave and a Starbucks, spends $6–9/day on coffee without flinching. Currently managing their café rotation with a combination of Google Maps, word of mouth, and memory.

*Where they hang out:* r/digitalnomad, r/remotework, r/Coffee, Hacker News, productivity Substacks, third wave coffee Instagram.

## Market Evidence

- Google Trends: searches for "coffee shops to work from [city]" at all-time highs
- Every major city subreddit has recurring threads: "where should I work from today?"
- Remote workers at cafés spend $150–$250/month — they're not price-sensitive, they're *option*-sensitive
- 44% of workers have "coffee badged" — physical café spaces are part of professional identity now
- Foursquare City Guide shut down December 2024 — the personal venue relationship layer is gone
- No app on Product Hunt, the App Store, or Google Play solves personal tracking + taste curation + habit journaling for this use case

## Competitive Landscape

| Competitor | What they do | What's missing |
|---|---|---|
| Google Maps / Yelp | Discovery at scale | No work-friendliness signal, no personal memory, no taste filter |
| Workfrom.co | Work-focused venue directory | Sparse data, no personal layer, feels abandoned |
| Foursquare / Swarm | Check-ins + personal history | Dead (city guide shut Dec 2024); no taste curation |
| Nomad Work / Nomadable | Digital nomad venue finder | Built for city-hoppers, not locals building a practice |
| Notes / Spreadsheets | Flexible personal tracking | No randomization, no discovery, no habit loop |

**The gap nobody fills:** taste-aware personalization with memory. Every competitor treats all venues as equal. Third Place knows *you*.

**Single differentiator:** Your preferences + your history + smart rotation = a tool built for locals, not tourists.

## Market Opportunity

- ~3–4M US remote workers use cafés regularly as work venues
- Realistic addressable audience (urban, specialty coffee, intentional): 500K–1M
- Already spending $1,800–$3,000/year on this behavior — app is managing the habit, not creating the spend
- Comparable lifestyle apps (Strava, Day One, Fantastical) price at $3–8/month or $25–50/year
- Lifestyle business at consumer tier: $180K–$300K ARR at 1% penetration (5,000 users)
- B2B expansion path: "third place operating system" for remote-first teams — sold as a company perk

## The Big Bet

That **a significant number of intentional remote workers will pay for a tool that manages their café practice** the same way runners pay for Strava to manage their running practice — not because they couldn't track it themselves, but because the structure, memory, and discovery loop makes the habit more satisfying and sustainable.

The personal version (Dan's tool, SF-scoped) is the proof of concept. The product version is the same app, multi-city, with onboarding tuned to taste preferences and a community discovery layer.
